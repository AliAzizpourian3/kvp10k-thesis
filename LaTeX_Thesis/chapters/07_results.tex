\section{Main Benchmark Results}

\subsection{Dataset Coverage}
\label{subsec:dataset_coverage}

The Stage 3 baseline evaluation was performed on the KVP10k test set. The original HuggingFace test split contains 5,255 annotated rows. However, our preparation pipeline (using PyMuPDF for native text extraction) implements the following deduplication and filtering:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Annotator deduplication:} The dataset contains approximately 5 annotator copies per unique page (identified by \texttt{hash\_name}). Deduplicating to unique pages yields \textasciitilde{}1,051 unique test pages.
    \item \textbf{Preparation success:} Of these 1,051 unique pages, only 581 were successfully prepared due to:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item PDF download failures (unreachable or corrupted URLs)
        \item PDFs lacking a usable text layer (PyMuPDF word extraction returns empty word lists for scanned documents)
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

Thus, our evaluation covers \textbf{581 unique test pages} ($\sim$55\% of unique pages, $\sim$11\% of raw HF rows), representing the maximum coverage achievable with PyMuPDF-based text extraction. This explains the gap compared to the full HF test set and is important to note when comparing to baselines that may use OCR-based text extraction (e.g., Tesseract).

\subsubsection{Per-Cluster Analysis: Answering "Why does Mistral Fail?"}
\label{subsec:per_cluster_analysis}

A deeper investigation into Stage 3 errors by layout type reveals a critical insight: \textbf{Mistral's performance is highly dependent on document density.} We classify test documents into two clusters based on layout density (number of entities per page area):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cluster 0 (Dense layouts):} $n=465$ documents with many entities per page
    \item \textbf{Cluster 1 (Sparse layouts):} $n=116$ documents with few key-value pairs per page
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{tab:per_cluster_errors} contrasts the error characteristics across these layout types:

\begin{table}[ht]
    \centering
0    \caption{Stage 3 Mistral Error Analysis by Layout Cluster (581 test pages)}
    \label{tab:per_cluster_errors}
    \begin{tabular}{lcccccc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Cluster} & \textbf{Docs} & \textbf{Pred} & \textbf{GT} & \textbf{Correct} & \textbf{Halluc.} & \textbf{Missed} \\
        & & \textbf{KVPs} & \textbf{KVPs} & \textbf{(\%)} & \textbf{(\%)} & \textbf{(\%)} \\
        \midrule
        Cluster 0 (Dense) & 465 & 7,852 & 8,161 & 48.7 & 40.5 & 31.2 \\
        Cluster 1 (Sparse) & 116 & 889 & 224 & 14.4 & 52.9 & 41.1 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent\textit{Note: Hallucination (\%) is the percentage of predicted KVPs that are unmatched in the ground truth, indicating spurious generation. For Cluster 1 (sparse), this high rate reflects fundamental over-prediction: 889 predictions vs.\ only 224 GT KVPs (3.97× over-generation).}

\textbf{Key Finding:} Mistral achieves a $3.4\times$ higher correct-match rate on dense layouts (48.7\%) compared to sparse layouts (14.4\%). This is attributable to several factors:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Catastrophic Over-Prediction on Sparse Layouts:} Sparse documents show 3.97× more predictions than ground truth (889 predictions vs.\ 224 GT KVPs). This leads to a hallucination rate of 52.9\% of predictions (or 209.8\% relative to GT KVPs), indicating that Mistral generates spurious key-value pairs even when few are actually present.
    
    \item \textbf{Distribution-Shift from Dense Training Data:} The model was trained primarily on dense-layout pages (majority of training corpus). Sparse pages present an out-of-distribution signal: fewer contextual clues, less recurring entity patterns. When confronted with few examples per page, Mistral reverts to generating hallucinated hypotheses rather than maintaining restraint.
    
    \item \textbf{Language Model Bias --- No Spatial Anchoring:} As a pure language model, Mistral has no inherent spatial reasoning or confidence calibration tied to layout density. Dense layouts provide rich textual context (many neighboring entities act as implicit signals), enabling the model to leverage co-occurrence patterns learned during training. Sparse layouts lack such signals, and the model defaults to template-driven hallucination.
    
    \item \textbf{Text vs. Spatial Trade-off:} Despite 52.9\% hallucination on sparse layouts, Mistral still extracts text with reasonable fidelity (text-only F1 achieves 0.67 even on sparse). However, the spatial grounding (bounding-box prediction) is weak across both layout types (text+bbox F1 = 0.60). This suggests Mistral can identify \textit{what} entities are present but struggles with \textit{where} they are, a limitation fundamental to its architecture.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent\textbf{Worst-Offender Analysis:} To understand where hallucination concentrates, we identify the 15 worst-offending documents by absolute hallucination count. Surprisingly, \textbf{all 15 belong to Cluster 0 (Dense)}, not the sparse cluster — with hallucination rates ranging from 59.6\% to 97.7\%. This reveals that hallucination is not exclusively a sparse-document phenomenon: dense, complex documents also cause Mistral to generate plausible but ungrounded KVPs, likely because high layout complexity without spatial grounding causes the model to confuse adjacent or visually proximate text regions as potential key-value pairs. Additionally, 42 documents with zero ground-truth annotations still received 324 predicted KVPs, confirming that Mistral generates output regardless of whether meaningful content is present. Both failure modes — complexity-driven hallucination in dense documents and content-free generation in empty documents — underscore the same root cause: absence of explicit spatial encoding.

\noindent\textbf{Research Implication:} This per-cluster breakdown and worst-offender analysis directly motivate the design of Stage 4: \textbf{LayoutLMv3 + Learned Linker}. LayoutLMv3 introduces layout-aware embeddings (visual position, relative location) that should mitigate hallucination by grounding predictions in spatial context. The dense-layout success of Mistral suggests that layout-aware multimodal models should perform even stronger on high-density documents, while both sparse and complex-dense documents present opportunities for improvement via explicit spatial grounding.
